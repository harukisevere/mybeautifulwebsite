japanese writing uses several types of symbols:

  • hiragana ひらがな
  • katakana カタカナ
  • kanji 漢字

let's talk about each in more details

kanji are chinese characters adapted to write japanese around the 3rd-5th centuries CE. kanji have two sounds — chinese on'yomi and japanese kun'yomi, both used in different cases. each kanji expresses a certain concept, carries its own meaning, and these concepts can become more complex if you put several kanji next to each other. this is an echo of the analytical nature of chinese writing. kanji make up the beginning, the unchangeable part of japanese words. there are a lot of kanji, and of course most japanese people cannot write even half of them from memory. the vocabulary of the average japanese person includes about 2,000 of them, and the most educated people know up to 5,000 kanji.

the character 水 means water; in the meaning of water, its japanese pronunciation (kun'yomi) — [mizu] is used; and in the word 水滴 — a drop of water — 水 is already read according to on'yomi — [sui]. 水曜日に水をたくさん飲んだ — [Suiyōbi ni mizu o takusan nonda] — I drank a lot of water on Wednesday.

hiragana is a syllabic alphabet that developed from сhinese cursive writing. hiragana symbols do not carry their own meaning and are used for recording the language by sound. оn average, they are used to write the "tails" (suffixes) of japanese words, but it can also be written exclusively with it: the less educated the intended readers of the text, the less kanji and more hiragana there are. it has a soft, smooth form, which is why in some texts it can even be used to emphasize female speech. unlike kanji, hiragana characters are limited to a small number of 48, and their diacritic variations: for example, か ka is read as が ga when the stressed diacritic ゛— handakuten — is added to the character.

私の黒い傘は美しい — [watashi no kurou kasa wa utsukushī] — "my black umbrella is beautiful". it can be written with hiragana only: わたくし の くろい かさ は うつくしい, but it's shorter, more readable and more beautiful to write it with kanji.

katakana is a syllabary that sounds exactly like hiragana, but is written differently. katakana literally means "fragmented kana", since its characters are formed from components of more complex kanji. the use of katakana syllabary is comparable to italics in english; it is mainly used to transcribe foreign words into japanese and to write loanwords, as well as for emphasis, to represent onomatopoeia, for technical and scientific terms, and for the names of plants, animals, minerals, and often japanese companies. japanese is a very non-conservative language, which is why it is very rich in loanwords written in katakana

in the picture below, right in the middle, there is the inscription カラオケ — karaoke. this word comes from オーケストラ [ōkesutora] — the japanese spelling of the english word "orchestra", so it is also written in katakana.

in addition to these three, in some cases the latin alphabet is used - romaji, but not as a separate script, but rather as a supplement to the existing ones. romaji is used on tourist signs, on the internet, of course in textbooks and in loanwords. for example, a t-shirt would be Ｔシャツ [tī shatsu], and j-pop would be Ｊポップ [jei poppu].

japanese grammar is very complex. it differs from most european languages because sentences in japanese are constructed according to the sov system — "subject, object, verb": 私は本を読みます [watashi wa hon o yomimasu], "i book read" — i read a book.
in addition, the grammar varies depending on the politeness level of the dialogue. for example, 丁寧語 (ていねいご, teineigo) — polite speech, applicable in everyday conversations with strangers and co-workers or clients — requires ending sentences with 〜ます (-masu) and 〜です (-desu); タメ口 (ためぐち, tameguchi) — informal style, allows omitting these and other particles and frequent use of slang. be not afraid! you won't really need it at first.

...however it's interesting! look: polite 「食べますか？」 (tabemasu ka?, "Would you like to eat?") in informal speech becomes 「食べる？」 (taberu?).
these are relatively minor changes, since in respectful speech (尊敬語, そんけいご, sonkeigo) the same sentence would look like 「お召し上がりになりますか」 (o-meshiagari ni narimasu ka?), which can be translated into english as a pompous "would you care to partake in a meal?".

let's put aside speech styles and look at the basics. sov system, according to which japanese sentences are built, was covered above. and what connects words in sentences are particles. here are the main ones:

thematic particle は (wa)
indicates the topic of the sentence. but for starters it will be more convenient to consider it as an english is.
「今日は曇りです」— today is (topic) cloudy (kyō wa kumori desu).
particle を (o)
is placed between the object of the action and the verb.
「電車を待っています」— i'm waiting for the train (densha o matteimasu).
interrogative particle か (ka)
if it is at the end of a sentence, the sentence becomes an interrogative one.
「音楽は好きですか？」— "do you like music?" (ongaku wa sukidesu ka?).

there are much more particles, but we'll keep them for now up to your further learning. and how can you learn japanese? here are a few options:

  1. duolingo — an app with daily exercises, including listening and even writing. it makes learning kana easy, and is a low-effort option for further learning.
  2. jisho — a dictionary that I use myself. it provides words in different spellings, example words in sentences, and even audio.
  3. kanji garden — an app that's designed for learning kanji, as the name suggests. it includes mnemonic phrases, example words containing kanji, on'yomi and kun'yomi readings, and animated stroke order diagrams.
  4. irodori — a series of free textbooks developed by the japan foundation. irodori is based on the shadowing method - simultaneous repetition of heard phrases. shadowing helps in developing listening and speaking skills, and also helps to get rid of an accent. the program also includes lessons on japanese customs, traditions, and social norms. starter textbook can be directly downloaded here.
